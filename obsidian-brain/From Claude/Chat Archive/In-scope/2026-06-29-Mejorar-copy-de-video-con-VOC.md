# Mejorar copy de video con VOC

> **Conversation overview**

Adri is a copywriter and brand strategist who works with founder clients, helping them develop personal brand content for social media. In this conversation, she used Claude to improve video scripts and carousel copy for a client named Luz (luzchapeton), a founder building her personal brand on Instagram. Adri's role was as the strategist directing the rewrites, not the content creator herself. Her working style is direct, paste-ready deliverables without gated follow-up questions, and she prefers markdown formatting compatible with Notion. She communicates with Claude in Spanish and Spanglish but her client's content is in English.

The session covered three distinct content pieces. First, a personal brand video about Luz's journey from 700 followers with a 90-day challenge to reach 10k. Adri's feedback centered on applying the VOC (Voice of Customer) rule — stripping generic coaching language and replacing it with falsifiable, human, first-person confession. Claude identified that the original copy failed because it used third-person framing ("they want to grow"), buried the most vulnerable and differentiated lines, and leaned on what Adri calls "guru-speak" — phrases like "sustainable system built around purpose" that no one would actually say. The rewrite centered the contradiction of being an expert at growing others' brands while her own sat at 700 followers. Second, a storytime-style video about Luz's first business — a pandemic-era bakery run with her mom — which Adri flagged as structurally broken because it opened chronologically and buried the core insight ("I didn't have to wait until I graduated to build something real") at the very end. Claude restructured it using the Proof of Concept / Give Back logic: the contradiction becomes the hook, the bakery lessons become compressed proof points, and the close teases a series rather than resolving everything. Third, a carousel with the Give Back pillar about five mistakes that kill a launch, based on Luz's real failed rebrand of a program. Adri's diagnosis was that the original was packaged as memoir (what happened to me) instead of actionable value (what you shouldn't do). Claude reframed each lesson from first-person narrative to second-person warning, collapsed the failure story into a single credentialing line on slide 2, and built the entire carousel toward a saveable cheat-sheet on slide 9.

Adri's stated preferences and corrections across the session: no over-formatting or heavy template structure in deliverables, always include two to three alternate hooks for testing, flag when real metrics or audience language would sharpen the copy further rather than inventing specifics, and treat the storytime and carousel skills as frameworks to apply intelligently rather than gatekeeping checklists. She also noted that for the carousel CTA, bridging to an owned property (YouTube, newsletter) would be stronger than a bare follow request, and asked whether Luz has such a destination — indicating she thinks about audience funnels beyond Instagram.

## 🤖 Claude · 2026-06-29 20:55
Le metí mano con el pressure test de copywriting. Antes de pegarte la versión nueva, el diagnóstico rápido de por qué el original no conecta:

**Lo que está matando la conexión**

- **Abre en frío.** "They want to grow… from 700 to 10k" arranca en tercera persona y con métrica. Nadie se queda por un número. Lo pasé a primera persona (era el cambio más importante para que suene personal).
- **Hay dos líneas de oro enterradas.** "It's easier to build someone else's brand than your own" y el gesto concreto de archivar todo. Eso es lo único que pasa la regla VOC tal cual está — es confesión real, no marketing. Todo lo demás las opaca.
- **Guru-speak que no pasa el filtro.** "build intentionally from the ground up", "sustainable system built around purpose", "every lesson, every mistake, every win". No las puedes visualizar, no las puedes falsificar, y las puede firmar cualquier coach. Fallan las 3 reglas.
- **Se va a modo instructor.** "that's what I've always taught founders to do" la pone por encima, no adentro. La fuerza emocional está en lo opuesto: es la experta que ahora está expuesta en la arena. Protagonista, no profesora.

**La versión reescrita (paste-ready, ~50 seg)**

> I grow personal brands for a living. My own account has 700 followers.
>
> And honestly? It's easier to build someone else's brand than your own. For years I've been hiding behind my clients' growth instead of doing the scary thing on my own page.
>
> So today I archived almost everything here. Clean slate.
>
> For the next 90 days I'm running the exact playbook I sell — out loud, where you can watch. 700 to 10k. 3 reels and 2 carousels a week. No "I'll post when I feel inspired." No going quiet for three weeks.
>
> You'll see what works. You'll see what flops. You'll see the days I don't want to hit publish.
>
> If this holds up, I prove my system works on the hardest client I've ever taken on — me.
>
> Day 1 is today. Follow and hold me to it.

**Por qué funciona:** abre con una contradicción falsificable (experta / cuenta de 700) que frena el scroll y la humaniza de una. "No 'I'll post when I feel inspired,' no going quiet for three weeks" es el "sustainable system" traducido a lenguaje real — mostrar lo que ES enseñando lo que NO es. Y "the hardest client I've ever taken on — me" convierte el problema de instructora en posicionamiento de protagonista.

**2 hooks alternativos** por si quieres testear la apertura (lo único que cambia es la primera línea):

- "I've helped founders hit 100k followers. I never did it for myself. Until today."
- "My job is building personal brands. Mine has been sitting at 700 followers, collecting dust."

Una cosa: esto lo afilé con el guion como única materia prima. Si me pasas comentarios o DMs reales de la audiencia de Luz —o de sus clientes founders— puedo hacer una pasada más quirúrgica y meter las palabras exactas que usa su gente. Pero la versión de arriba ya te queda lista para grabar.

## 🤖 Claude · 2026-06-29 21:14
Ahí está el problema de fondo: el video es **lineal**. Setup → desarrollo → lección al final. Y la única frase que se gana el watch — "no tenía que esperar a graduarme para construir algo mío" — es literalmente lo último que dice. Casi nadie llega ahí. Para un Proof of Concept eso es fatal: la prueba tiene que ir adelante, no de premio.

**Qué está enterrado / qué falla**

- **Abre carraspeando.** "As a founder, I've had several businesses, I want to talk about…" es etiqueta + cronología. Cero gap de curiosidad. La regla del storytime es justo esa: prohibido el inicio cronológico.
- **La contradicción que ES el hook está implícita y al fondo.** Aprendió costos, márgenes e inventario vendiendo postres en la cocina de su mamá. Esa contradicción (cookies → negocio real) es exactamente tu línea de "I learned how to run my business selling cookies." Va arriba.
- **La prueba se cuenta como lista plana.** "Costs, margins, inventory and all those things that are super important" — eso es *decir*, no *probar*. No lo puedo visualizar ni falsificar.
- **No hay teaser ni loop abierto.** Suelta la historia entera y la lección más profunda en 40 segundos. No queda nada por lo cual seguirla. Justo el payoff-teaser que dijiste que falta.

**Cómo se reordena**

La frase del final ("no tenía que esperar a graduarme") deja de ser el cierre y se vuelve el **payoff con pausa**. La contradicción (postres → fundamentos de negocio) sube a **hook**. Y las lecciones reales (pricing, inventario) se convierten en el **loop abierto**: las prometes como serie, no las dumpeas. Eso es el teaser.

**Script reescrito (paste-ready, ~50 seg, en su voz):**

> I didn't learn business in college. I learned it selling cake out of my mom's kitchen.
>
> It was the pandemic. Sixth semester, bored out of my mind, stuck at home — and my mom was just as bored as I was. So we started baking. Cakes, pies, ice cream. All handmade, all sold straight from our kitchen.
>
> And that tiny dessert business taught me what no class did. How to price something so you actually make money. How to not run out of ingredients in the middle of an order. How much a "cheap" cake really costs you once you count every single egg.
>
> *(pausa, a cámara)* But the real lesson wasn't the margins. It was realizing I didn't have to wait until I graduated to build something real — I already was.
>
> So if you've got a whole notes app of ideas you keep saving for "when you're ready" — I waited too. Until a cake stand changed my mind.
>
> I'll break down exactly what selling cake taught me about pricing, inventory, and not going broke. But that's a series. Follow so you don't miss it.

**2 hooks alternativos** para testear la apertura:

- "Everyone told me to wait until I graduated to start a business. I didn't listen — and a cake stand proved me right."
- "My first real business lesson didn't come from college. It came from a cake order I almost lost money on."

Una cosa que dispara el Proof of Concept directo a otro nivel: si Luz tiene **un número real** del bakery — cuánto facturó, cuántos pedidos, el precio de un pastel, las semanas que duró — metemos eso en el hook y deja de ser anécdota para volverse caso con evidencia. Ej: *"the little dessert business that did $X in three months taught me more than my degree."* Si me lo pasas, te reescribo el hook con la cifra adentro.

## 🤖 Claude · 2026-06-29 21:24
El empaque, exacto. El problema no son las ideas — son oro y se quedan. El problema es que está **envuelto como memoir**: slides 1 a 4 son "lo que construí / lo que gasté / cómo me sentí". El espectador no se lleva nada usable hasta la slide 5. Para un pilar Give Back eso está al revés: **el valor va adelante, y el fracaso se reduce a la credencial** — la prueba de que te ganaste las lecciones, no el tema del post.

Entonces hice esto: cada lección pasó de "esto me pasó a mí" → "esto no lo hagas tú". Una regla por slide. El relanzamiento fallido vive en **una sola línea** (slide 2) para que tenga peso, y todo el carrusel se diseña hacia el **save de la slide 9**, que es el verdadero regalo. Perfil: **Analista** (densidad alta, promesa de herramienta).

Tus líneas más fuertes ("trust doesn't carry over just because you've sold before", "slow cooker, not a microwave") las dejé tal cual — esas ya pasan VOC solitas.

**SLIDE 01 — Hook (promesa de herramienta)**
> "5 things that quietly kill a launch — before anyone even sees your offer."

*Visual: texto grande sobre fondo limpio. Nada de foto tuya acá — esto promete una herramienta, no presenta a alguien.*

**SLIDE 02 — Contexto + credencial (la única línea sobre ti)**
> "I found all 5 the expensive way. Team hired, ads running, full rebrand — and on launch day? Silence. It wasn't the algorithm. It was these."

*Visual: puede ir una captura del "0 ventas" o un gráfico plano. La prueba en pantalla, no tu cara.*

**SLIDE 03 — #1**
> "Don't rebrand to feel fresh. New name, new energy, same content — it doesn't make you stronger. It erases the recognition you already earned and resets trust to zero."

**SLIDE 04 — #2**
> "Don't assume past buyers will buy again. Trust doesn't carry over just because you've sold before. Every launch you re-earn it — or you launch to no one."

**SLIDE 05 — #3**
> "Don't try to buy your way past demand. A team and paid ads amplify trust you already have. They can't manufacture it. Spending more just burns money faster."

**SLIDE 06 — #4**
> "Don't build the pretty side while ghosting the people who buy from you. The logo, the funnel, the aesthetic — none of it sells if your audience stopped hearing from you. They forget you. Fast."

**SLIDE 07 — #5**
> "Don't sell to an audience you haven't built yet. Content is a slow cooker, not a microwave. You can't skip the part where people get to know you — and then ask them to buy."

**SLIDE 08 — A-ha (la línea que une las 5)**
> "Every one of these is the same mistake: chasing the launch instead of the relationship. People don't buy the offer. They buy the person they've watched long enough to trust."

*Visual: esta tiene que funcionar como cita suelta, screenshoteable.*
