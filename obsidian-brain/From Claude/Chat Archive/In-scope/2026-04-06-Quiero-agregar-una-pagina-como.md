# Account Health Check Framework

## Who This Is For

This framework works regardless of your role or business model. Whether you're a brand owner running your own ads, a creative strategist auditing a client account, a solo ecommerce entrepreneur, or a personal brand monetizing through digital products or affiliate links — the process is the same. What changes is the data source you pull from.

---

## Your Data Sources by Business Type

Before you start, identify which platforms you pull data from. You don't need all of them. You need the ones that show you where attention is going, where money is coming in, and where people are dropping off.

**Ecommerce Brands**
- Shopify Analytics
- Meta Ads Manager
- TikTok Ads Manager
- Google Ads / Google Analytics 4
- Amazon Storefront (if applicable)
- Walmart Creator Center (if applicable)
- Triple Whale / Northbeam / Motion *(if available)*

**Digital Product Creators & Info-Business**
- Gumroad
- Stan Store
- Kajabi
- Payhip
- Beacons
- Hotmart
- Meta / TikTok Ads Manager *(if running paid)*

**Content Creators & Affiliate-First Businesses**
- LTK (LikeToKnowIt)
- Amazon Storefront
- TikTok Creator Center
- Linktree Analytics
- Instagram Insights / TikTok Analytics
- YouTube Studio *(if applicable)*

**All Business Types**
- Meta Ads Manager
- TikTok Ads Manager
- Google Analytics 4
- Platform-native analytics (wherever your audience lives and converts)

---

## The Framework

---

### Step 1: Define Your Baseline

Before you can assess health, you need to know what healthy looks like for your specific account. This step is about establishing your benchmarks — not industry averages, your numbers.

**What to do:**

Pull the last 60–90 days of data from your primary ad platform and your conversion source. You're looking for:

- **Average CPA (Cost Per Acquisition)** — what you're currently paying per sale, lead, or conversion
- **Average ROAS or MER** — return on ad spend or blended marketing efficiency ratio if you have multiple channels
- **Average CTR** — click-through rate on your ads, separated by placement if possible
- **Average Hook Rate** — percentage of people who watched past the first 3 seconds *(video only)*
- **Average Hold Rate** — percentage of people who watched 25%, 50%, 75% of your video *(video only)*
- **Conversion Rate on landing page or product page** — how many of the people who clicked actually bought or converted

**By data source:**

- *Shopify:* Pull conversion rate, average order value, and top traffic sources
- *Meta Ads Manager:* Pull CPA, ROAS, CTR, CPM, frequency, and video metrics
- *TikTok Ads Manager:* Pull the same metrics plus video completion rate
- *Gumroad / Stan Store / Kajabi / Payhip / Hotmart / Beacons:* Pull total sales, conversion rate per product, and traffic source breakdown
- *LTK / Amazon Storefront:* Pull click volume, conversion rate, and top performing products
- *TikTok Creator Center:* Pull top videos by views and engagement to identify organic content that could translate into paid
- *Linktree:* Pull click-through rate per link to identify which offers or destinations are actually getting attention

**Why it matters:**

Most accounts skip this step and go straight to optimization. Without a baseline, you're optimizing blind. You don't know if a 2% CTR is good or bad for your account until you know what your account has historically produced.

---

### Step 2: Audit Your Creative Inventory

Now you look at what you're actually running. This step maps your existing creative library against the pillar framework so you can see how your spend is distributed and where the gaps are.

**What to do:**

Pull your top 10–15 creatives by spend over the last 60–90 days. For each one, log:

- **Pillar** — what strategic territory does this ad play in?
- **Concept** — what is the specific testable idea?
- **Angle** — what consumer motivation or tension is it tapping into?
- **Format** — UGC, static, motion graphic, talking head, slideshow, etc.
- **Hook type** — confession, bold claim, question, before/after, etc.
- **Performance** — CPA, ROAS, CTR, and video metrics if applicable

Once logged, group by pillar and look at the distribution.

**By data source:**

- *Meta Ads Manager:* Use the Creative Reporting breakdown. Filter by spend, sort descending, export top performers
- *TikTok Ads Manager:* Use Creative Center or the campaign-level creative breakdown
- *Motion / Triple Whale:* If available, these tools make this step significantly faster with automated creative tagging

**Why it matters:**

Most accounts accidentally over-index on one pillar. They find one thing that works — usually Social Proof or Problem/Solution — and pour everything into it. That creates concentration risk. When that pillar fatigues, the entire account drops. A healthy account has tested creative across at least 3–4 distinct pillars.

---

### Step 3: Identify Concentration Risk and Untested Territory

This step is the direct output of Step 2. Once you have your creative inventory mapped, two problems become visible immediately.

**Concentration risk** means the majority of your spend is sitting inside one or two pillars. The account is being carried by a single creative territory. This is fragile. When the winning creative fatigues — and it will — there's nothing behind it.

**Untested territory** means entire pillars have zero or one concept inside them. These are your next testing opportunities. Not because every pillar needs to be filled, but because untested territory is where your next winner is hiding.

**What to do:**

Build a simple pillar map. You can do this in a spreadsheet, a Notion table, or even on paper:

| Pillar | # of Concepts | % of Spend | Top Performer |
|---|---|---|---|
| Social Proof | 4 | 62% | The Skeptic Converted |
| Problem/Solution | 2 | 28% | The Hidden Cost |
| Education | 1 | 10% | How It Works |
| Founder Story | 0 | 0% | — |
| Lifestyle/Aspiration | 0 | 0% | — |

A table like this tells you immediately: Social Proof is carrying this account. Founder Story and Lifestyle haven't been tested at all. That's your roadmap.

**Why it matters:**

This is the difference between an account that reacts to creative fatigue and one that anticipates it. You always want to be testing the next pillar before the current one peaks.

---

### Step 4: Diagnose Funnel Breakdown Points

Now you shift from creative to conversion. This step looks at where people are dropping off between seeing the ad and completing the purchase or desired action.

**What to do:**

Map your funnel from impression to conversion and identify the biggest drop-off point:

- **High CPM, low CTR** → The ad is being shown but not clicked. The hook or creative isn't stopping the scroll.
- **High CTR, low landing page conversion** → People are clicking but not buying. The problem is on the page, not the ad.
- **High add-to-cart, low purchase** → People want it but something is stopping them. Price, trust, shipping cost, or checkout friction.
- **High purchase rate, low ROAS** → The economics aren't working. AOV is too low or CAC is too high relative to LTV.

**By data source:**

- *Meta Ads Manager:* Compare CTR (link) vs. landing page views vs. add-to-cart vs. purchases
- *Shopify:* Check checkout abandonment rate and cart abandonment rate
- *Google Analytics 4:* Use the funnel exploration report to visualize drop-off by step
- *Kajabi / Gumroad / Stan Store / Hotmart / Payhip:* Check your sales page conversion rate and compare traffic source quality
- *LTK / Amazon Storefront:* Compare click volume to actual purchases — a high click, low conversion rate usually means product-page friction or pricing misalignment
- *Linktree:* If you're driving traffic through a link-in-bio, check which destination links are converting vs. which are getting clicks but no follow-through

**Why it matters:**

Most people try to fix a conversion problem with more creative. But if the drop-off is happening on the landing page or at checkout, no amount of better hooks will fix it. You have to diagnose before you prescribe.

---

### Step 5: Assess Audience Saturation and Frequency

This step looks at whether your ads are reaching new people or burning out on the same audience.

**What to do:**

Check your frequency and CPM trends over the last 30–60 days.

- **Frequency above 3.0** in a cold audience campaign is a signal of saturation. People have seen the ad enough. Either the audience is too small or the creative rotation is too thin.
- **Rising CPM with flat or declining CTR** means the algorithm is working harder to find new people and the ones it's finding are less responsive. Classic fatigue signal.
- **Declining ROAS over time with no changes to the offer** is almost always a creative or audience saturation problem.

**By data source:**

- *Meta Ads Manager:* Check frequency at the ad set level and CPM trends over time using the date comparison feature
- *TikTok Ads Manager:* TikTok doesn't show frequency the same way — monitor CPM and video completion rate trends instead
- *Triple Whale / Northbeam:* If available, these tools show blended frequency and cross-channel overlap

**Why it matters:**

Saturation is invisible until it's already hurting you. Monitoring it proactively lets you rotate creative or expand audiences before performance drops.

---

### Step 6: Score and Prioritize

The final step is turning everything you found into a prioritized action list. Not everything needs to be fixed at once. You need to know what to move on first.

**Scoring by urgency:**

| Issue | Priority |
|---|---|
| Funnel breakdown (drop-off at a specific step) | 🔴 Fix first |
| Concentration risk (one pillar carrying the account) | 🔴 Fix first |
| Creative fatigue / high frequency | 🟡 Fix next |
| Untested pillars | 🟡 Fix next |
| Weak baseline metrics with no clear cause | 🟢 Monitor |

**What to do:**

Write a one-paragraph account summary that covers:

1. Where the account is healthy
2. Where the biggest risk is right now
3. What the next 2–3 actions are, in order

This summary becomes your strategic brief — whether you're handing it to a client, a creative team, or using it yourself to plan the next sprint.

A step-by-step diagnostic you can apply to any account — regardless of business model, platform, or experience level.

### Step 1: Define Your Baseline

Before you can assess health, you need to know what healthy looks like for your specific account. This step is about establishing your benchmarks — not industry averages, your numbers.

**What to do:**

Pull the last 60–90 days of data from your primary ad platform and your conversion source. You're looking for:

- **Average CPA** — what you're currently paying per sale, lead, or conversion
- **Average ROAS or MER** — return on ad spend, or blended marketing efficiency ratio if you have multiple channels
- **Average CTR** — click-through rate on your ads, separated by placement if possible
- **Average Hook Rate** — percentage of people who watched past the first 3 seconds *(video only)*
- **Average Hold Rate** — percentage of people who watched 25%, 50%, 75% of your video *(video only)*
- **Conversion rate on landing page or product page** — how many of the people who clicked actually converted

**By data source:**

- *Shopify* → conversion rate, average order value, and top traffic sources
- *Meta Ads Manager* → CPA, ROAS, CTR, CPM, frequency, and video metrics
- *TikTok Ads Manager* → same metrics plus video completion rate
- *Gumroad / Stan Store / Kajabi / Payhip / Hotmart / Beacons* → total sales, conversion rate per product, and traffic source breakdown
- *LTK / Amazon Storefront* → click volume, conversion rate, and top performing products
- *TikTok Creator Center* → top videos by views and engagement to identify organic content that could translate into paid
- *Linktree* → click-through rate per link to identify which offers or destinations are actually getting attention

Most accounts skip this step and go straight to optimization. Without a baseline, you're optimizing blind. You don't know if a 2% CTR is good or bad for your account until you know what your account has historically produced.

### Step 3: Audit Your Creative Inventory

Now you look at what you're actually running. This step maps your existing creative library against the pillar framework so you can see how your spend is distributed and where the gaps are.

**What to do:**

Pull your top 10–15 creatives by spend over the last 60–90 days. For each one, log:

- **Pillar** — what strategic territory does this ad play in?
- **Concept** — what is the specific testable idea?
- **Angle** — what consumer motivation or tension is it tapping into?
- **Format** — UGC, static, motion graphic, talking head, slideshow, etc.
- **Hook type** — confession, bold claim, question, before/after, etc.
- **Performance** — CPA, ROAS, CTR, and video metrics if applicable

Once logged, group by pillar and look at the distribution of spend across territories.

**By data source:**

- *Meta Ads Manager* → use the Creative Reporting breakdown, filter by spend descending, export top performers
- *TikTok Ads Manager* → use Creative Center or the campaign-level creative breakdown
- *Motion / Triple Whale* → if available, these tools make this step significantly faster with automated creative tagging

**Why it matters:**

Most accounts accidentally over-index on one pillar. They find one thing that works — usually Social Proof or Problem/Solution — and pour everything into it. That creates concentration risk. When that pillar fatigues, the entire account drops. A healthy account has tested creative across at least 3–4 distinct pillars.

### Step 4: Spot Concentration Risk and Untested Territory

This step is the direct output of Step 3. Once you have your creative inventory mapped, two problems become visible immediately.

**Concentration risk** means the majority of your spend is sitting inside one or two pillars. The account is being carried by a single creative territory. This is fragile — when the winning creative fatigues, there's nothing behind it.

**Untested territory** means entire pillars have zero or one concept inside them. These are your next testing opportunities. Not because every pillar needs to be filled, but because untested territory is where your next winner is hiding.

**What to do:**

Build a simple pillar map in a spreadsheet or Notion table:

| Pillar | # of Concepts | % of Spend | Top Performer |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Social Proof | 4 | 62% | The Skeptic Converted |
| Problem / Solution | 2 | 28% | The Hidden Cost |
| Education | 1 | 10% | How It Works |
| Founder Story | 0 | 0% | — |
| Lifestyle / Aspiration | 0 | 0% | — |

A table like this tells you immediately: Social Proof is carrying this account. Founder Story and Lifestyle haven't been tested at all. That's your roadmap.

**Why it matters:**

This is the difference between an account that reacts to creative fatigue and one that anticipates it. You always want to be testing the next pillar before the current one peaks.

---

### Step 5: Diagnose Funnel Breakdown Points

Now you shift from creative to conversion. This step looks at where people are dropping off between seeing the ad and completing the purchase or desired action.

**What to do:**

Map your funnel from impression to conversion and identify the biggest drop-off point:

- **High CPM, low CTR** → the ad is being shown but not clicked; the hook or creative isn't stopping the scroll
- **High CTR, low landing page conversion** → people are clicking but not buying; the problem is on the page, not the ad
- **High add-to-cart, low purchase** → people want it but something is stopping them — price, trust, shipping cost, or checkout friction
- **High purchase rate, low ROAS** → the economics aren't working; AOV is too low or CAC is too high relative to LTV

**By data source:**

- *Meta Ads Manager* → compare CTR (link) vs. landing page views vs. add-to-cart vs. purchases
- *Shopify* → checkout abandonment rate and cart abandonment rate
- *Google Analytics 4* → use the funnel exploration report to visualize drop-off by step
- *Kajabi / Gumroad / Stan Store / Hotmart / Payhip* → sales page conversion rate and traffic source quality
- *LTK / Amazon Storefront* → click volume vs. actual purchases; high click / low conversion usually means product page friction or pricing misalignment
- *Linktree* → which destination links are converting vs. which are getting clicks but no follow-through

**Why it matters:**

Most people try to fix a conversion problem with more creative. But if the drop-off is happening on the landing page or at checkout, no amount of better hooks will fix it. You have to diagnose before you prescribe.

---

### Step 6: Assess Audience Saturation and Frequency

This step looks at whether your ads are reaching new people or burning out on the same audience.

**What to do:**

Check your frequency and CPM trends over the last 30–60 days:

- **Frequency above 3.0** in a cold audience campaign is a signal of saturation — the audience is too small or the creative rotation is too thin
- **Rising CPM with flat or declining CTR** means the algorithm is working harder to find new people and the ones it's finding are less responsive — a classic fatigue signal
- **Declining ROAS over time with no changes to the offer** is almost always a creative or audience saturation problem

**By data source:**

- *Meta Ads Manager* → check frequency at the ad set level and CPM trends over time using the date comparison feature
- *TikTok Ads Manager* → TikTok doesn't show frequency the same way; monitor CPM and video completion rate trends instead
- *Triple Whale / Northbeam* → if available, these tools show blended frequency and cross-channel overlap

**Why it matters:**

Saturation is invisible until it's already hurting you. Monitoring it proactively lets you rotate creative or expand audiences before performance drops.

---

### Step 7: Score and Prioritize

The final step turns everything you found into a prioritized action list. Not everything needs to be fixed at once — you need to know what to move on first.

**Scoring by urgency:**

| Issue | Priority |
| --- | --- |
| Funnel breakdown at a specific step | 🔴 Fix first |
| Concentration risk (one pillar carrying the account) | 🔴 Fix first |
| Creative fatigue / high frequency | 🟡 Fix next |
| Untested pillars with no concepts | 🟡 Fix next |
| Weak baseline metrics with no clear cause | 🟢 Monitor |

**What to do:**

Write a one-paragraph account summary that covers:

1. Where the account is healthy
2. Where the biggest risk is right now
3. What the next 2–3 actions are, in order

This summary becomes your strategic brief — whether you're handing it to a client, a creative team, or using it yourself to plan the next sprint.

## Glossary of Terms

**AOV — Average Order Value**
The average amount a customer spends per transaction. Calculated by dividing total revenue by the number of orders in a given period. A higher AOV means each purchase is worth more, which gives you more room to spend on acquisition.

**CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost**
The total amount spent to acquire one paying customer. Includes ad spend and any other marketing costs tied to getting that customer in the door. A sustainable business acquires customers for significantly less than what those customers will generate over their lifetime.

**CPM — Cost Per Mille**
The cost per 1,000 impressions. Mille is Latin for thousand. It measures how much you're paying every time your ad is served 1,000 times, regardless of whether anyone clicks. A rising CPM signals that it's getting more expensive for the algorithm to find people to show your ad to — usually a sign of audience saturation or creative fatigue.

**CTR — Click-Through Rate**
The percentage of people who saw your ad and clicked on it. Calculated by dividing clicks by impressions. A strong CTR means your hook and creative are compelling enough to earn the click. A weak CTR means the ad is being shown but not acted on — the problem is in the creative, not the landing page.

**CPA — Cost Per Acquisition**
The amount you spend to generate one conversion — whether that's a purchase, a lead, a sign-up, or any other defined action. Your CPA is one of the clearest indicators of whether your paid media is profitable at the current volume.

**ROAS — Return On Ad Spend**
The revenue generated for every dollar spent on ads. Calculated by dividing revenue by ad spend. A ROAS of 3x means you earned $3 for every $1 spent. ROAS is a useful efficiency metric but doesn't account for all business costs — which is why many brands also track MER.

**MER — Marketing Efficiency Ratio**
A blended efficiency metric that looks at total revenue divided by total marketing spend across all channels, not just one platform. Unlike ROAS, MER gives you a more accurate picture of how your marketing investment is performing as a whole, especially when running ads on multiple platforms simultaneously.

**Hook Rate**
The percentage of people who watched past the first 3 seconds of your video ad. It measures how effectively your opening moment stops the scroll and earns attention. A low hook rate means people are swiping past before your message even starts.

**Hold Rate**
The percentage of people who continued watching your video past a certain point — typically measured at 25%, 50%, and 75% of the total duration. It tells you how well your content sustains attention after the hook. A high hook rate with a low hold rate means you earned the click but lost them in the body of the ad.

**LTV — Lifetime Value**
The total revenue a single customer generates over the entire time they remain a customer — not just their first purchase. Also written as CLV or CLTV. A business with strong LTV can afford to spend more to acquire each customer because the relationship doesn't end after one transaction.

**LTV:CAC Ratio**
The relationship between what a customer is worth over time and what it cost to acquire them. A healthy benchmark is 3:1 or higher — meaning for every dollar spent acquiring a customer, you should expect to get at least three back over their lifetime. Below that ratio, growth becomes expensive and fragile.

**Frequency**
The average number of times a single person has seen your ad within a given time period. A frequency above 3.0 in a cold audience campaign is typically a signal of saturation — the audience is too small or the creative rotation is too thin. High frequency leads to creative fatigue and rising CPMs.

**Creative Fatigue**
The point at which an ad has been shown so many times to the same audience that performance starts to decline. Symptoms include rising CPM, falling CTR, and declining ROAS over time with no changes to the offer or landing page.

**Concentration Risk**
A structural vulnerability in your creative strategy where the majority of your ad spend is sitting inside one or two pillars. If that pillar fatigues, there's nothing behind it to sustain performance. A healthy account distributes testing across multiple pillars.

**Pillar**
The broadest strategic container in your creative framework. It groups your creative work by theme or approach — the territory your ads are playing in. Examples: Social Proof, Education, Problem/Solution, Founder Story.

**Concept**
A specific, testable creative idea that lives inside a pillar. It combines an audience, a message, a format, and a hook into one coherent bet. A single pillar can contain many concepts, each distinct enough to succeed or fail on its own.

**Angle**
One component within a concept. The specific consumer motivation or reason-to-buy being communicated. It answers: what tension, desire, or pain point is this ad tapping into? Multiple concepts can share the same angle but execute it differently through format, hook, and audience.

# How Meta's Algorithm Works

Understanding the system behind ad delivery is not optional for anyone running paid media on Meta. The algorithm determines who sees your ads, how often, at what cost, and whether your creative gets amplified or ignored. This page breaks down the three AI systems that power Meta's ad delivery, what they actually do, and what that means for your strategy.

---

## The Big Picture

Meta's ad delivery system is a three-stage AI pipeline. Every time a user opens their feed, this pipeline runs in milliseconds to decide which single ad to show them out of billions of candidates.

| Stage | System | What It Does |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Retrieval | Andromeda | Scans billions of ads and narrows down to ~1,000 candidates |
| 2. Foundation | GEM | The central brain — provides learned patterns to inform ranking |
| 3. Ranking | Lattice | Takes the ~1,000 candidates and selects the single best ad to serve |

**The pipeline flow:**

USER REQUEST → ANDROMEDA (billions → ~1,000) → GEM (knowledge + patterns) → LATTICE (~1,000 → 1 ad served)

**The most important thing to understand about this entire system:**

Creative signals are the primary input to every stage of this pipeline. Andromeda uses creative embeddings to retrieve candidates. GEM learns patterns from creative engagement. Lattice ranks based on predicted relevance shaped by creative quality. This is why Meta says: *"Creative is the new targeting."*

---

## System 1: Andromeda (The Retrieval Layer)

### What is Andromeda?

Andromeda is Meta's machine learning system for ad retrieval. It is the first gate your ad has to pass through before it can even be considered for delivery. Its job is to scan billions of ads and narrow them down to approximately 1,000 candidates per user request — in real time, every time someone opens their feed.

Think of Andromeda as the librarian of a library with billions of books. When a user walks in, Andromeda doesn't read every book — it uses a sophisticated indexing system to quickly identify which ~1,000 books are most relevant to that specific person at that specific moment.

### How does it work?

Andromeda uses **embedding-based retrieval**. An embedding is a mathematical representation of a piece of content — in this case, your ad. Andromeda converts your ad into an embedding and stores it in a hierarchical index. When a user request comes in, Andromeda generates an embedding for that user based on their history and context, then searches the index for ads whose embeddings are mathematically closest to the user's embedding.

This is not keyword matching. It's not interest targeting. It's semantic similarity — the system is asking: *"Which ads are most conceptually aligned with what this person cares about right now?"*

**The retrieval process step by step:**
1. User request arrives with context — device, time, placement, recent behavior
2. Andromeda generates a user embedding based on history and context
3. Hierarchical index is searched for semantically similar ad embeddings
4. ~1,000 candidates are selected based on embedding proximity
5. Candidates are passed to GEM and Lattice for ranking

### The Entity ID System

Andromeda groups similar ads under the same **Entity ID**. Ads with the same Entity ID compete for the same delivery pool — meaning they reach the same audience and cannibalize each other's reach rather than expanding it.

**What does NOT count as a distinct creative:**

| "Diversity" Attempt | Why It Fails |
| --- | --- |
| Same video with different text overlay | Same visual = same Entity ID |
| Same footage with different voiceover | Same visual = same Entity ID |
| Multiple hooks saying the same thing differently | Same message = same Entity ID |
| Re-cut edits of the same shoot | Same footage = same Entity ID |
| Minor caption or music changes | Cosmetic change only |

**What DOES count as a distinct creative:**

| True Diversity | Why It Works |
| --- | --- |
| Different camera angles or locations | New visual signal |
| Human-focused vs. product-only | Different subject |
| New emotional tone (funny vs. serious) | Different creative signal |
| Entirely different message or offer | New intent signal |
| Different creator or talent | New visual identity |
| Different format (static vs. video) | Different consumption pattern |

### What this means for you as an advertiser

Before Andromeda, targeting levers — interests, behaviors, lookalikes — were the primary way advertisers controlled who saw their ads. Andromeda changed that fundamentally.

- **Creative diversity is now mandatory.** Similar creatives get similar embeddings, which means they reach similar audiences. If you want to reach different people, you need fundamentally different creative — not variations of the same concept.
- **Targeting levers matter less.** Andromeda decides who sees your ads based on learned patterns from your creative signals, not from the audience boxes you check.
- **Volume matters.** More truly diverse creatives means more embedding coverage, which means more audience reach.
- **Quality signals compound.** High engagement trains better embeddings for your ads, which improves future delivery.
- **High Creative Similarity = higher CPMs.** If your creative library lacks diversity, Andromeda algorithmically penalizes your account by raising your CPMs because it treats your content as repetitive. The fix is not more variations — it's more fundamentally different creative.

---

## System 2: GEM (The Foundation Model)

### What is GEM?

GEM stands for **Generative Ads Recommendation Model**. It is Meta's foundation model for ads recommendation — described by Meta as *"the central brain"* of their advertising AI system. It is the largest foundation model for recommendation systems in the industry, trained at the scale of large language models across thousands of GPUs.

A **foundation model** is a large AI model trained on massive amounts of data that serves as the knowledge base for many smaller, specialized models. Think of GEM as the senior strategist who has studied every campaign that has ever run on Meta — and then passes that accumulated knowledge down to hundreds of junior specialists, each responsible for a specific platform or objective.

GEM doesn't serve ads directly. Instead, it transfers its knowledge to hundreds of **vertical models** — specialized models that actually serve ads across Meta's platforms (Facebook Feed, Instagram Feed, Reels, Stories, etc.).

### What does GEM learn from?

GEM trains on three types of data:

| Data Type | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Ad content data | Creative assets, copy, format, offer |
| User engagement data from ads | Clicks, conversions, video views, saves |
| Organic interaction data | Non-ad content behavior across Facebook and Instagram |

That last point is critical: **GEM learns from organic content, not just ads.** How a user interacts with organic Reels informs how GEM predicts their likelihood of engaging with an ad. Your audience's behavior everywhere on Meta feeds into the system.

### Cross-platform learning

GEM learns from cross-surface user interactions while maintaining platform-specific optimization. In practice this means: a user's Instagram Reels behavior informs their Facebook Feed ad relevance, and vice versa. The knowledge flows across all surfaces — but predictions remain tailored to each platform's unique characteristics.

### What this means for you as an advertiser

- **Your creative signals train GEM.** Every engagement pattern your ads generate — clicks, video completions, saves, purchases — is data that GEM learns from. Good creative doesn't just convert today; it trains the system to find more people like your converters tomorrow.
- **Cross-platform behavior matters.** Instagram performance informs Facebook delivery. Running on multiple placements gives GEM more signals to work with.
- **Organic content matters.** GEM learns from non-ad interactions too. A strong organic presence on the same surfaces where you run ads gives the system more context about the type of person who engages with your brand.
- **Compounding effects are real.** Good performance trains better future predictions. Accounts with a history of strong creative engagement get progressively better delivery over time — and accounts with weak creative history have to work harder to recover.

---

## System 3: Lattice (The Ranking Layer)

### What is Lattice?

Lattice is Meta's unified ranking framework — the final decision-maker in the pipeline. After Andromeda retrieves ~1,000 candidate ads, Lattice evaluates all of them and selects the single ad to serve to that user at that moment.

Before Lattice existed, Meta ran hundreds of separate deep neural network models — one for each platform, objective, and placement combination. Lattice replaced all of them with a single unified architecture that shares knowledge across every domain simultaneously.

Think of Lattice as the final judge in a competition. It receives 1,000 candidates from Andromeda, consults the knowledge GEM has provided, and makes the final call — balancing what the advertiser wants (conversions, clicks, views) with what the user wants (relevant, non-annoying ads) and what Meta wants (platform health and revenue).

### What does Lattice optimize for simultaneously?

This is what makes Lattice different from older ranking systems — it doesn't optimize for a single objective. It balances multiple objectives at once:

- Clicks
- Video views
- Conversions
- User satisfaction
- Ad quality
- Platform health

This is called **Multi-Domain, Multi-Objective (MDMO) learning.** It's why running low-quality ads that generate clicks but frustrate users will eventually hurt your delivery — Lattice is penalizing you on the user satisfaction and ad quality dimensions even if your CTR looks fine.

### Built for the post-iOS14 world

Lattice was specifically designed to handle data scarcity — the reality after iOS14 and privacy changes reduced Meta's access to granular user-level tracking data. Instead of relying on individual user tracking, Lattice leverages patterns learned across all domains to make predictions even when direct data is limited.

This means: even when Meta can't track a specific user precisely, Lattice can still make strong predictions by borrowing patterns from similar users across all its platforms. **Privacy changes hurt less sophisticated systems more than they hurt Lattice.**

### What this means for you as an advertiser

- **Creative quality is ranked, not just creative relevance.** Lattice balances advertiser objectives with user satisfaction and platform health simultaneously. An ad that gets clicks but generates negative feedback is being penalized on dimensions you can't see directly.
- **Cross-platform performance compounds.** Instagram engagement helps Facebook predictions. Reels performance informs Feed delivery. The better you perform across surfaces, the more data Lattice has to rank you favorably.
- **Multiple objectives are always being balanced.** Optimizing purely for conversions without considering creative quality and user experience will eventually surface in your CPMs and delivery.

## The Three Systems Working Together

| System | Role | Input | Output |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Andromeda | Retrieval | Billions of ads + user context | ~1,000 candidates |
| GEM | Foundation Model | Creative signals + engagement history + organic behavior | Learned patterns + embeddings |
| Lattice | Ranking | ~1,000 candidates + GEM knowledge | 1 ad served |

**The data flow:**
1. User request arrives with context
2. Andromeda retrieves ~1,000 candidate ads based on embedding similarity
3. GEM provides learned patterns and knowledge to inform ranking
4. Lattice ranks candidates across multiple objectives simultaneously
5. Final ad is served

**The strategic implication of understanding all three systems together:**

Your creative is not just an ad. It is the primary input signal to a three-stage AI system that determines your reach, your costs, and your results. Every creative decision — format, message, angle, visual treatment, volume, diversity — is a signal you are sending to this pipeline. The advertisers who understand this build creative strategies accordingly. The ones who don't wonder why their CPMs keep rising.

## Creative Diversification & The Motivators Framework

### The core principle

With Andromeda determining who sees your ads based on creative signals — not manual audience targeting — creative diversification is no longer a best practice. It is the primary mechanism for reaching different audiences.

*"Creative is the new targeting."*

Meta's data on creative diversity:

| Finding | Data |
| --- | --- |
| Ad sets with 3–10 creatives vs. 1 creative | Outperformed with 99.99% confidence |
| Median CPA for multi-creative ad sets | 46% lower |
| Creative influence on Estimated Action Rates | +/- 50% |
| Recommended minimum volume | 10 unique creative assets |
| Recommended optimal volume | 20–30 unique creative assets |

### The Motivators Framework

Motivators are the underlying psychological drivers that make someone take action. The same product can appeal to completely different people for completely different reasons. A single creative angle only reaches the segment of your audience that resonates with that specific motivation.

| Motivator | What It Does | Example Hook |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pain Point | Addresses a frustration or problem | Stop wasting time on a 10-step routine |
| Pleasure / Aspiration | Promises a desired outcome | Wake up with glowing skin every day |
| Social Proof | Borrows trust from others' validation | Join 50,000+ happy customers |
| Curiosity | Creates an information gap | The ingredient dermatologists won't tell you about |
| Fear / Urgency | Drives immediate action | Sale ends tonight |
| Identity | Speaks to who they are | Made for busy moms who don't have time |
| Feature-Led | Highlights specific attributes | Made for sensitive skin |
| Problem / Solution | Classic before/after structure | Tired of X? Here's how to fix it |
| Authority / Expert | Leverages external credibility | Doctor-recommended |
| Comparison | Positions against alternatives | Why this beats your current solution |

### The Five Dimensions of Creative Diversification

True creative diversity happens across five dimensions simultaneously. Changing one dimension while keeping the others identical is not enough.

**Dimension 1: Different Storylines**
Founder origin story, day-in-the-life, problem/solution, things you didn't know, behind-the-scenes, testimonial/review, unboxing/first impression.

**Dimension 2: Different Formats**
UGC-style selfie video, static with bold headline, split-screen comparison, animated infographic, Reels-style montage, carousel, collection ad.

**Dimension 3: Different Value Propositions**
Time savings, cost efficiency, premium quality, convenience, results and efficacy, sustainability, exclusivity.

**Dimension 4: Different Emotional Tones**
Inspirational, relatable/problem-first, urgent/limited-time, calm/reassuring, humorous/satirical, educational, emotional/heartfelt.

**Dimension 5: Different Audience Personas**
Busy professionals, first-time parents, fitness enthusiasts, budget-conscious buyers, luxury seekers, skeptics.

### The Diversification Formula

> **PERSONA × MOTIVATOR × FORMAT = Truly diverse creative library**

Example: 3 personas × 3 motivators × 3 formats = 27 unique ad concepts from a single product.

### What to monitor

- **Creative Fatigue** — performance declining on a specific creative over time
- **Creative Similarity Score** — how similar your active ads are to each other; high similarity raises CPMs algorithmically
- **Top Creative Themes** — where spend is concentrating; this tells you which pillar is carrying the account

### Weekly production target (high-spend accounts)

5 core concepts × 2–3 formats each = 10–15 truly diverse assets per week

# Metrics Cheat Sheet

CPA and ROAS tell you if the entire journey is efficient or not — they account for the full funnel including auction price. Every other metric below helps tell the story of *how* people are interacting with your brand throughout that journey, and where the specific opportunities to iterate and optimize are.

## How to read this document

Each metric is organized around the **Job To Be Done (JTDB)** — the specific stage of the customer journey it measures. For each metric you'll find:

- **What it is** — the definition
- **What to do when it's low** — specific iteration opportunities

## Meta Metrics

---

### Stage 1: Capture Attention
*Ad element: Hook (First 1–3 seconds) — Video only*

**1st Frame Retention**
The percentage of people that allow your video to start playing in their feed.

*If below 90%:* Change the first frame of your asset. Try including a text overlay that helps users know what to expect in the ad. Test branded vs. unbranded first seconds to see which captures more attention. Does the first frame feature a face or the product?

---

**Thumbstop Ratio**
The percentage of people watching the first 2–3 seconds of your video.

*If lower than average:* Iterate on your hook. Try different audio, text overlays, and visuals.

---

### Stage 2: Hold Attention
*Ad element: Post Hook (4s to end of video) — Video only*

**Thruplay (15s views / impressions)**
The percentage of people watching the first 15 seconds of your video. This tells you if you're holding attention after the hook.

*If lower than average:* Try changing the content right after the hook (4s–8s). Note: high CTR assets may have a lower Thruplay if people are clicking out early — that's not necessarily bad. Use the video drop-off chart in Creative Insights to pinpoint where users are falling off.

---

**15s / 3s Video Retention**
The percentage of people that watched the first 3 seconds of your video and continued watching to the 15-second mark. This qualifies your hook — it shows not just who stopped, but who stayed.

*If lower than average:* Which hooks pull in more engaged users? What content keeps them watching? Analyze the 4s–15s section to find hypotheses. Potential iterations: create a clear storyline from hook to 15s mark, or increase the pace of the asset.

---

**100% Video Plays (Rate)**
The percentage of views where the user watched 100% of the video.

*If lower than average:* Change up content to keep people's attention all the way to the end. Note: high CTR can make this metric lower — that's actually a good thing since you want people clicking through to your site.

---

**Video Avg. Play Time**
The average number of seconds people are watching your video. Make sure your most important content — offer, key benefit, CTA — falls within this window.

*If lower than expected:* Does your offer or key benefit fall after this point? Pull it up earlier. Focus on changing the content at the average second watched to try to increase it. Iterating on your hook can also change the quality of people you reach, which affects this metric.

---

**Thumbstop Avg. Watch Time**
The average seconds watched by those who watched at least 3 seconds.

---

### Stage 3: Get People to Site
*Ad element: Call to actions — copy, scripting, text overlays*

**CTR (All)**
The percentage of people that liked, shared, commented, or clicked out on your ad.

*If lower than expected:* High CTR (all) signals high engagement and typically drives lower CPMs. Monitor in combination with CTR (link) to make sure people aren't just engaging but actually making it to your site. You can increase engagement by asking questions in your videos, covering topics worthy of discussion, and interacting with people in your comment sections.

---

**CTR (Link Clicks) / CTR (Outbound)**
The percentage of people that clicked to an external link from your ad.

*If lower than expected:* Share more about what they can expect when clicking to your site. Change up your copy, scripting, and text overlays to communicate the reason to click. Why should someone stop scrolling to visit your site right now?

---

**Thumbstop CTR**
The percentage of people that watched the first 2–3 seconds of your video and clicked through to your site. This qualifies your hook — it shows which hooks drive action vs. just capturing attention.

*How to use it:* Compare Thumbstop CTR with Thumbstop Ratio. If there's a big gap between the two, people are stopping but not clicking — the hook is capturing attention but not creating enough intent to act. This is more common in retargeting campaigns.

---

### Stage 4: Drive Engagement on Site
*Ad element: Landing page and site experience*

**Bounce Rate** *(requires Google Analytics)*
The percentage of people that left your site without visiting a second page.

*If high:* Update the landing page experience to encourage the user to explore further. Note: a high bounce rate isn't always bad — if the goal is simply to get people to visit a specific landing page, it may be acceptable.

---

**Time on Site** *(requires Google Analytics)*
The average seconds spent on your site after clicking from the ad.

*If low:* Qualify your CTRs by seeing how long people typically spend on site. Low time on site may mean you need to update your landing page strategy, or refine your hooks and messaging to reach more qualified users.

---

**Pages Viewed**
The average number of pages viewed per session.

---

**Leading Indicators of Purchase**
Actions like lead submissions, blog views, or specific product page views that signal intent before a purchase happens.

---

**Click to Action Funnel (Visits → Product Views → Add to Cart)**
The percentage of people that clicked to your site and took a specified action at each step of the funnel.

*How to use it:* Find the drop-off points in the journey. Are people viewing the product page but not adding to cart? Are they adding to cart but not purchasing? Evaluate the experience at each step to identify what's stopping them from continuing.

---

### Stage 5: Win the Purchase
*Ad element: Site experience*

**Click to Purchase Ratio (Purchase CVR)**
The percentage of people that clicked to your site and completed a purchase.

*If low:* A low conversion rate can indicate issues with the site experience or that buyers need more education or time before committing. Find the biggest blockers for conversion and address them in your ad creative and landing pages.

---

**AOV — Average Order Value**
The average value of an order placed.

*How to increase it:* A higher AOV directly improves your ROAS. Try changing your landing page experience, offering bundles or upsell opportunities, and featuring more products in your ads if you have multiple SKUs. For single-SKU brands: is there a reason someone would buy two? Try messaging that to encourage the double purchase.

*How to increase it:* A higher AOV directly improves your ROAS. Try bundles, upsell opportunities, featuring more products in your ads. For single-SKU brands: is there a reason someone would buy two? Try messaging that.

---

**Value of Complete Payments**
The average order value as reported directly inside TikTok Ads Manager.

---

**Click to Purchase Ratio (Purchase CVR)** *(requires Northbeam)*
The percentage of people that clicked to your site and completed a purchase.

*If low:* Indicates issues with the site experience or that buyers need more education before committing. Address the biggest conversion blockers in your ad creative and landing pages.

---

**Complete Payment %**
Out of all users who clicked on the ad, the percentage who completed payment as reported inside TikTok Ads Manager.
