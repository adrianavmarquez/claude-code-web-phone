## 🤖 Claude · 2026-06-24 13:48
Most of this transcript is luxury-coded. Diana's buyer is someone whose stated problem is losing standing among other rich people. Your Client A is a creator with 2K followers stuck on the hook. If you lift her lines as-is, you get exactly the hollow imitation your Anti-Strategy series exists to mock. So I filtered for the mechanics that survive the jump to your audience, translated them, and flagged the ones that should stay in the luxury box.

The gold, ranked by short-form potential.

**1. Delay the how.**
Her exact move: "I'm not educating, I'm leading, and I bury the education later." People scroll past the how-to, so she leads with identity and aspiration, agitates, and only hands over the mechanic when they're almost begging for it.
This is the most short-form-native idea in the whole episode. A retention tactic wearing a philosophy costume.
Angle: a video that does the thing while explaining the thing. You withhold your own "how" until second 40 and name out loud that you're doing it.

**2. Storytelling is contrast, not the hero's journey.**
"How did I feel before, how do I feel after." That's the entire engine. Works in 300 pages or two sentences. No mentor, no underworld, no 12 steps.
Kills a belief half your audience is paralyzed by: that they need a Pixar arc to post anything.
Angle: take a "boring" before/after and show it beats a full story structure for a 30-second clip.

**3. Mirror + Vision = Desire.**
Mirror their language back with zero shame and you earn trust. Show them where they could be and you open the gap. The gap is the desire.
A copy formula you can hand someone. Relatable-only content has no pull. Aspirational-only content shames. The mechanism is the combo.
Angle: break down a caption that does only one half, then add the missing half on screen.

**4. What they think is the problem is a symptom.**
"You think it's a pricing problem, it's actually a messaging problem." Diagnose before you prescribe.
This is the spine of your Video Diagnóstico already. Same logic, different surface.
Angle: a "you don't have a consistency problem" teardown that reveals the real bottleneck underneath.

**5. Don't tell them what to do.**
The four moves she runs on clients: diagnose with a question, reiterate their problem in the most charitable version, put the blame on something external (the website, not them), then offer your idea as a question instead of a prescription. Nobody wants their agency attacked.
Sell-without-selling in four concrete steps. Cuadra perfecto con TCF.
Angle: the four moves as a talking head, or a carousel where slide 09 is the "question, not prescription" reveal.

**6. The research is the work. The words are the residue.**
Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce line took three to four weeks of research. "Just Do It" is three words. Copywriters undersell themselves charging per word when the value is the digging.
Your anti "ChatGPT writes my hooks" position, with an outside credibility anchor.
Angle: the iceberg. Three words above the water, three weeks below.

**7. Mood and setting before the product.**
She never opens on notes or features. She situates you first ("the hour before dawn"), then the product, then ties it back to identity ("she returns home at last"). Two lines, full arc.
Direct hook construction. Most creators open on the feature. The scene is the hook.
Angle: rewrite a feature-first opening into a scene-first one, live, same product.

**8. Vocabulary builds trust. It isn't there to look smart.**
Speaking the insider language of a niche signals "I'm in the same rooms you are." Cultural fluency, and trust comes before any sale.
For Client A picking a lane: generic language reads as outsider, specific language reads as one of us.
Angle: the same idea written generic vs written in-niche, side by side, and which one you'd trust.

**9. Start niche, then expand.**
"Go too wide too quickly and you're just a mass market brand." Control who you sell to, build a loyal core, widen from there.
Positioning correction for early creators chasing reach before they have a center.
Angle: why "content for everyone" lands with no one.

**10. AI makes bad copywriters obsolete and strategic thinkers more valuable.**
Her words. The work moves to positioning, differentiation, and articulating a POV. The wordsmithing gets commoditized.
Your whole 2026 thesis, confirmed by a voice that isn't yours.
Angle: what AI still can't do, the diagnosis and the POV, shown not told.

## Developed and ready to copy

### LinkedIn — long post (ideas 1 + 2, your flagship industry take)

Ogilvy spent close to a month researching one car before he wrote a single line for it. The line that came out sold Rolls-Royces for years.

That ratio is the whole job. Weeks of digging for one sentence of output.

Most people price it backwards. They charge for the sentence and give the research away, which is how an entire craft talked itself into looking cheap.

Then AI showed up and did the sentence in four seconds.

Here is what that actually kills: the writer whose only skill was assembling words. That role is gone and it should be. What it cannot touch is everything that happened before the writing. The diagnosis. The reason this exact buyer wants this exact thing. The point of view that decides what to leave out.

A luxury strategist put it cleanly on a podcast recently: AI makes bad copywriters obsolete and strategic thinkers more valuable. (Diana Nicola, Kensington Copy.)

The people panicking were selling the residue. The people getting more expensive were always selling the dig.

If your work can be replaced by a prompt, the prompt was never the problem.

### LinkedIn — short post (idea 5, for strategists and creators)

The reason your educational content dies at the 40-second mark: you led with the lesson.

People do not scroll for instructions. They scroll for identity. The how-to is the thing they say they want and the thing they abandon the second you hand it over.

Lead with who they become if this works. Sit in that long enough that they feel the gap. Release the mechanic only when the wanting is louder than the boredom.

I am doing it to you right now. You are still reading because I have not given you the steps. That tension is the format.

### X — thread (idea 4, the strongest one for your strategist lane)

**1/**
Nobody high-agency takes orders. Founders, creators, anyone good at what they do. The second you say "you need to," you've lost them.

Here's how you move someone without attacking their agency. Four moves I borrowed from a luxury strategist and run on every advisory call.

**2/**
Diagnose with a question, not a statement.

"What's your acquisition looked like lately?" beats "your problem is acquisition."

One opens the door. The other makes them defend the room they're standing in.

**3/**
Say their problem back to them in the most generous version that's still true.

They need to hear that you understood it before they'll believe you can fix it. Trust comes before the prescription. Every time.

**4/**
Put the blame on something outside of them.

Not "you priced this wrong." The market shifted. The website buried it. A force they didn't control.

Now it's the two of you against the problem instead of you against them.

**5/**
Offer your idea as a question.

"What if we tested a new page at a 10% higher price?" lands. "You should raise your prices" bounces.

Same idea. One they own, one they resent.

**6/**
Knowing the answer is the easy part. Getting someone to arrive at it themselves, and feel like they did, is the entire job.

(Framework adapted from Diana Nicola, Kensington Copy.)
