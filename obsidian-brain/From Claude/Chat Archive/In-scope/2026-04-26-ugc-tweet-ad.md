## 🤖 Claude · 2026-04-26 14:29
Now let me think through the ad context from the image carefully before writing.

**What I see in the UGC tweet:**
- Creator: @yourgalsasha
- Product: Calmobility "Blue Calm Bites" (gummies in a cobalt blue jar)
- Claim: Panic attacks have "basically stopped" after taking these, almost 6 months anxiety-free
- Format: Screenshot of an organic tweet used as a paid ad (social proof screenshot format)

This is a **social proof UGC tweet ad.** The copy that runs around it (primary text, headline) needs to amplify the testimonial, not compete with it. The tweet IS the creative. The copy frames why you should believe it and what to do next.

**Persona:** Women 25-40 dealing with chronic anxiety, panic attacks, tried therapy, maybe meds, looking for a natural daily support option.

**Awareness stage:** Problem-aware to solution-aware. They know they have anxiety. They may be skeptical of supplements but are open.

**The tension to resolve in copy:** "Can a gummy actually stop panic attacks?" The tweet claims yes. Copy needs to make that believable without sounding like a drug claim.

---

Here are ad copy options written for this format. Each includes primary text (above the tweet), headline (below the image/CTA area), and a brief note on the angle.

---

## Option 1: Social Proof Amplifier (Problem-Aware)

**Primary text:**
"She wasn't sure it would work either.

6 months later, her panic attacks are basically gone.

Calmobility Blue Calm Bites. Real ingredients. Real people. Real results."

**Headline:** "Try it risk-free. See why thousands are finally breathing easier."

*Angle: Neutralizes skepticism ("she wasn't sure either") before the reader even voices it. Keeps the testimonial as the emotional center.*

---

## Option 2: Identity Call-Out + Pain Agitation (Problem-Aware)

**Primary text:**
"If anxiety is running your life, this is worth 30 seconds of your time.

Real person. 6 months. Panic attacks gone.

Not a prescription. Not a lifestyle overhaul. Just one thing that actually helped."

**Headline:** "Try Calmobility Blue Calm Bites. Free shipping on your first order."

*Angle: Speaks directly to the overwhelmed anxiety sufferer who is tired of complicated solutions. "Not a prescription, not a lifestyle overhaul" is a key differentiator against the alternatives they've already tried.*

---

## Option 3: Curiosity Gap (Solution-Aware, leans skeptical)

**Primary text:**
"People keep asking what finally worked for her anxiety.

It's not what you'd expect.

6 months. No panic attacks. One small change."

**Headline:** "See what she's been taking. Shop Calmobility."

*Angle: Withholds the product name in the copy so the tweet visual does the reveal. Creates a natural open loop. Works best if the tweet screenshot is clearly visible.*

---

## Option 4: Short and Direct (Most-Aware or Retargeting)

**Primary text:**
"Still anxious every day?

She said the same thing 6 months ago."

**Headline:** "Try Blue Calm Bites. 30-day money back guarantee."

*Angle: Maximum brevity. The tweet carries all the emotional weight. Copy just creates a before/after frame in two sentences. Best for warm audiences who've already seen the brand.*

---

**Recommendation:** Start with Option 2 for cold audiences. Option 4 for retargeting. The copy should never try to out-explain the tweet. The tweet is the proof. Copy is just the frame that makes the proof land.
