## Summary

In this Week 2 class of the Motion 2026 Creative Strategy Bootcamp, Sarah Levinger (Founder, Tether Insights) presents a psychology-based research framework organized around three "realities": Customer Reality (generational insight, micro-moments, emotional mapping), Brand Reality (health and alignment), and Presentation Reality (the T-E-E-P creative framework: Trigger, Exploration, Evaluation, Purchase). Sarah emphasizes that research must serve a specific decision, that avatars are a crutch without micro-moment specificity, and that creative format choice should map to the customer's psychological state. Evan Lee introduces the session, recaps Week 1, walks through the Week 2 homework, and demonstrates Motion's Brand Intel, Boards, AI chat (Runneth), and the new "Grade My Hooks" tool.

## Frameworks / models / taxonomies presented

- **Three Realities Research System** (Sarah's own) — Customer Reality → Brand Reality → Presentation Reality → Strategic Creative Brief. Introduced ~13:06.
- **Customer Reality Cake (3 layers)** — Generational Insight (bottom), Micro-Moments (middle), Emotional Mapping (top). Introduced ~19:15.
- **Four Generational Questions** — (Q1) Childhood informational environment, (Q2) Relationship with money, (Q3) Where they are in real life right now, (Q4) How they evaluate risk and decide who to trust.
- **Three Emotional Mapping Questions** — Which emotion is present? Private or social moment? Acute or chronic struggle?
- **Brand Reality (2 parts)** — Health (what can we produce, cost, time, who) + Alignment (what we're aiming for, what we're not, who owns it, how we know we hit it).
- **Cost Iceberg** — Above water: spend. Below water: dev time, labor, bandwidth, ops load.
- **Alignment Statement template** — "For the next [timeframe] we are optimizing for [specific goal] because [business reason]."
- **T-E-E-P Creative Framework** (Sarah's own) — Trigger / Exploration / Evaluation / Purchase, each at 25% as a baseline; reweighted for TOF (40/20/20/20), CVR (10/40/40/10), Cost reduction (10/35/35/20).
- **Valence and Intensity Model** — Attributed to psychologist James A. Russell (1980); 2x2 quadrant of emotion (positive/negative) × intensity (low/high). Sarah applies it to ad selection.
- **Customer Journey Loop** — Trigger → Exploration ↔ Evaluation → Purchase → Experience. Attributed to a Google study on digital purchasing.

## Tactics recommended (do this)

- Before opening any research tool, ask: "What decision will this research help me make?" If you can't answer, close the browser.
- Build a tagged, browsable ad library categorized by T-E-E-P phase (Trigger / Exploration / Evaluation / Purchase).
- Once a month, ask every team member (founder, media buyer, creative, designer): "If you could only optimize for one thing right now, what would it be?" — write down verbatim, look for disagreement.
- Get a one-sentence Alignment Statement committed for a defined window (30/60/90 days).
- Research customer generational context across four questions: childhood info environment, money relationship, current life stage, risk/trust patterns.
- Map specific micro-moments (real-world situations, not marketing situations) where the problem becomes salient — these become ad scripts.
- For each micro-moment, run the three emotional mapping questions to determine *how* to communicate.
- Tag every saved inspiration ad with its T-E-E-P phase so the library becomes browsable by customer state.
- Use Reddit, Facebook Groups, Quora, niche forums for first-person micro-moment language.
- Use Pew Research Center for generational worldview; Bureau of Labor Statistics / Census for cultural-economic forces.
- Use Amazon reviews (compare 5-star, 3-star, 1-star) and Trustpilot for emotional language and objection-finding.
- Spend at least 30 minutes a week consuming organic content in your feed for presentation/format research.
- Optimize creative weighting based on goal: TOF=trigger-heavy (40%), CVR=evaluation+purchase-heavy, Cost reduction=quality middle of funnel.
- For TOF goals, talk to your media buyer about link-click / traffic optimization (not conversion).
- For small teams: prioritize customer research and presentation research; skip generational research first.
- Use AI agents to scrape real conversations (TikTok, Twitter, Instagram) for keywords rather than asking LLMs to assume insights.
- Validate team production capacity ("at quality") before pitching strategy volume.
- Check ops/inventory dashboards before scaling demand-driving creative.

## Anti-patterns (don't do this)

- Don't gather research without a destination — it becomes Alice-in-Wonderland wandering.
- Don't write down "angles" during generational research — absorb into a mental library instead.
- Don't rely on demographic/aspirational avatars alone (e.g., "Minimalist Melissa, 34, Whole Foods").
- Don't research for "fun facts" — research for deep insights tied to a specific moment.
- Don't pitch a creative volume your team can't physically produce at quality.
- Don't assume team alignment on optimization goals — establish it.
- Don't dump tons of low-quality creative; quality > quantity.
- Don't copy trending formats blindly — filter through customer psychological state.
- Don't write generic situations, generic pain points, or generic benefits (Tylenol, Owala, IKEA examples shown by Evan as what to avoid).
- Don't have a media buyer optimizing for clicks while strategy is built for LTV — alignment must start there.
- Don't keep talking past the purchase moment — let the customer buy.
- Don't have AI conduct large-scale research that defaults to its bias (e.g., always returning psychology) — direct it specifically.
- Don't assume CTR = conversions; click is not a customer.
